# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

ios

## Users

The person who records. They speak memories into the physical Storeybox Box at home, and this app is where they return to revisit what they said. They open it in quiet, unhurried moments — a chair, an evening — rather than on the go. The Box lives in their home; the app is the archive of that room, carried in their pocket.

## Product Purpose

Storeybox Home is the companion archive for the Storeybox Box. The Box records; the app does not. The app pairs the Box to an account, reflects its status, and turns what the Box brings home into a browsable archive of Storeys — playback, transcript, summary, themes, people, emotional texture, provenance.

Success looks like two things. First, a revisiting ritual: the owner returns regularly to listen, wander the archive, and rediscover moments — time in the app is the point. Second, moments worth sharing: a Storey makes its way to someone else, played at a dinner table or sent to a sibling.

## Positioning

Memory lives at home, and the archive writes itself. Recording happens in the room, on a dedicated object — never through a phone — and a single spoken moment becomes transcript, summary, themes, people, and emotional texture with zero effort from the speaker. Every screen should reinforce both halves: the app is the quiet reflection of a physical object, and the order it shows was never work.

## Brand Personality

Tender, nostalgic, handcrafted. The app should read like a well-kept family daybook — something made with care, holding things that matter — never like software administering recordings.

Reference points, each for a specific quality: Hatch Restore, the closest situational analog — a calm hardware object in the home with a quiet companion app that serves it. Lapse and Retro, for memory-keeping with deliberately withheld gratification and no public metrics. Teenage Engineering and Analogue, for hardware-object brands where the physical thing carries the identity and the software honors it. Day One, for the ritual cadence of entries accumulating into a life record.

## Anti-references

- A voice-memo utility (Otter, Voice Memos): functional lists of recordings led by waveforms, timestamps, and file management.
- A social feed: Instagram-style grids, engagement mechanics, streaks, notification pressure.
- Smart-home gadget UI: device stats, connectivity widgets, and setup flows dominating the experience.
- Generic SaaS minimalism: interchangeable cream-and-sans dashboard styling that could belong to any product.

## Design Principles

1. The Box records; the app remembers. The app never competes with the hardware for the act of capture — no microphone UI, no recording affordances. Its job is to make what the Box brought home feel kept.
2. Withheld, not gamified. No streaks, counts-as-achievements, or engagement pressure. Rediscovery is paced and quiet; the archive rewards returning, it never demands it.
3. Order without effort. Themes, people, textures, and summaries appear as if the archive organized itself. Never ask the user to file, tag, name, or maintain anything to keep the archive coherent.
4. A daybook, not a dashboard. Editorial, printed-page presentation leads every screen; device status, sync state, and settings stay subordinate and small.
5. Made for unhurried hands. Generous type, large targets, no time pressure, nothing hidden behind gestures a first-time or older user wouldn't find.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Comfortable for older users: generous type sizes with Dynamic Type support, large touch targets, strong contrast, and reduced-motion alternatives. Spoken-memory keepers often skew older; the app should feel easeful at 70, not just at 30.
